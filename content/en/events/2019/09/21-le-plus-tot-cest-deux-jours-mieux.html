Titled "LE PLUS TÔT C’EST DEUX JOURS MIEUX" (THE SOONER IS TWO BETTER), according to the Breton proverb 'N abretañ ar gwellañ (in French: Le plus tôt c'est toujours mieux)", Seulgi Lee’s exhibition at La Criée contemporary art center presents a set of works nourished by close or distant collaborations. It explores the notion of trope, a figure of speech leading to a change or diversion of meaning.

body: >-

About

The artist borrows the notion of trope from Richard Sennett*.
Defining craftsmanship beyond a specialized knowledge, the sociologist
reevaluates its fundamental contribution to the development of
practices, but also of human theories. Richard Sennett's approach finds
an echo in the work of Seulgi Lee, who has been working closely with
artisans for several years, in an attempt to make visible the link
between craftsmanship and oral culture.**. The title is the first
trope of the exhibition: the gap in meaning is linked here to a shifted
apprehension of the proverb by the artist, who plays with humour on his
strange relationship with the French language. We also find the figure
of speech in the group of works U, of which six covers are presented at
La Criée. On these, geometric compositions are created in the
traditional technique of Nubi, each featuring a proverb widely used in
Korea. Two diversions occur simultaneously via the imaged processing of
the language and the symbolic power of the drawings. Thus, in 짚신도 짝이 있다.
Jip-sin-do Jjak-i It-da 
( Même la sandale en paille trouve sa paire ) (Even the straw
sandal finds its pair), which means 'a soulmate exists for everyone',
one can indeed see two sandals in the coloured fabric ovals of the cover
that overlap slightly. The interest in oral transmission leads
Seulgi Lee to take an interest in the intangible culture of the French
regions through their repertoires of traditional songs. The two films
presented in the exhibition bear witness to this. The first, titled DEPATTURE ,
very close to the documentary, collects songs and testimonies of
singers from Poitou, driven by their taste for singing as much as by the
defense of their repertoire. Echoes the fiction ÎLE AUX FEMMES , filmed this summer in Trégor, in which two young women sing and dance in the twilight that thickens. Seulgi
Lee's exhibition at La Criée blurs the boundaries and operates multiple
shifts from craft to art, from oral transmission to its fixation, from
the universal to the singular, from the immemorial past to the fleeting
contemporary. The artist transforms La Criée by color and brings
together for the first time blankets from Tongyeong, shamans papers from
Mount Gyeryong or Jeju Island in Korea, Mexico's Ixcatec basketry,
Morocco's Riffian pottery, or songs from the countries of Gargantua,
punctuated by two large painted metal stabiles - abstract and giant
representations of female sexes. Seulgi Lee proposes to "fold the space
of La Criée and then slowly unfold it in order to make the lights of
twilight (again) come out".
* Richard Sennett, Ce que sait la main : la culture de l’artisanat , Albin Michel, 2010 ** Seulgi Lee, correspondance avec Pierre Déléage, 17 juillet 2019

The artist borrows the notion of trope from Richard Sennett*. Defining craftsmanship beyond a specialized knowledge, the sociologist reevaluates its fundamental contribution to the development of practices, but also of human theories. Richard Sennett's approach finds an echo in the work of Seulgi Lee, who has been working closely with artisans for several years, in an attempt to make visible the link between craftsmanship and oral culture.**.

The title is the first trope of the exhibition: the gap in meaning is linked here to a shifted apprehension of the proverb by the artist, who plays with humour on his strange relationship with the French language. We also find the figure of speech in the group of works U, of which six covers are presented at La Criée. On these, geometric compositions are created in the traditional technique of Nubi, each featuring a proverb widely used in Korea. Two diversions occur simultaneously via the imaged processing of the language and the symbolic power of the drawings. Thus, in 짚신도 짝이 있다. Jip-sin-do Jjak-i It-da  (Même la sandale en paille trouve sa paire) (Even the straw sandal finds its pair), which means 'a soulmate exists for everyone', one can indeed see two sandals in the coloured fabric ovals of the cover that overlap slightly.

The interest in oral transmission leads Seulgi Lee to take an interest in the intangible culture of the French regions through their repertoires of traditional songs. The two films presented in the exhibition bear witness to this. The first, titled DEPATTURE, very close to the documentary, collects songs and testimonies of singers from Poitou, driven by their taste for singing as much as by the defense of their repertoire. Echoes the fiction ÎLE AUX FEMMES, filmed this summer in Trégor, in which two young women sing and dance in the twilight that thickens.

Seulgi Lee's exhibition at La Criée blurs the boundaries and operates multiple shifts from craft to art, from oral transmission to its fixation, from the universal to the singular, from the immemorial past to the fleeting contemporary. The artist transforms La Criée by color and brings together for the first time blankets from Tongyeong, shamans papers from Mount Gyeryong or Jeju Island in Korea, Mexico's Ixcatec basketry, Morocco's Riffian pottery, or songs from the countries of Gargantua, punctuated by two large painted metal stabiles - abstract and giant representations of female sexes. Seulgi Lee proposes to "fold the space of La Criée and then slowly unfold it in order to make the lights of twilight (again) come out".
